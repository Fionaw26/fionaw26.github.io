Breather

In New York City, trying to send text messages between underground train stops is an extreme sport. Every second feels like a competition. Parents rush to catch the M train at Delancey, scooters in hand, with two little ones running behind with mini backpacks bouncing on their shoulders. Teenagers sprint after the m9, hoping the light stays red enough for them to cross, thanking the bus driver that waited and cursing the ones who don’t. However, just a few blocks away, the Asian elders of Chinatown move at their own pace. They sit on the benches of Columbus Park, sipping tea from thermoses, playing poker, and chatting as the city races around them. It’s as though their collection of schemas had allowed them to perceive a truth that the youth are unable to grasp. Everywhere I look, the city moves. Cars honking down Grand Street, trains rattling beneath the pavement, footsteps echoing against the sidewalk, and voices buzzing in every language imaginable. New York never stops. Even during downtime, weekends, days off, people hurry between family dinners and museum dates, always trying to fit everything in.

I remember one summer afternoon walking through the Lower Eastside during my lunch break. The warmth of the sun and the occasional breeze brushed gently against my skin, the air thick with the smell of roasted nuts from a nearby cart. A woman sat outside of a coffee shop on 2nd Avenue, sipping an iced latte while typing away on her laptop. In the middle of the street stood a crossing guard, ensuring the safety of drivers, road workers, and pedestrians. Everyone seemed to know exactly where they needed to be. I, on the other hand, still had twenty-seven minutes of break left. And so, I wandered. A homeless man lay still on a flattened piece of cardboard beside the 14 Street Union Square station, a sign and a clear plastic cup in front of him. The same 2-by-2 Cafe I’ve seen all over my Instagram feed looked smaller in person, its neon sign flickering faintly above the window. What seemed like forever turned out to be only eleven minutes. It felt as though time was slowing down for once. Restaurants lined the streets, with menus proudly displayed in their windows. I stopped in front of a thrift store I’d always wanted to visit, only to find a large red Closing Sale banner draped across the glass. Has anyone noticed how quickly the city changes? One moment a store was opening, the next moment it was replaced by something newer, trendier, and perhaps faster. With seven minutes remaining, I made my way back to work. The red light stops the traffic momentarily, and for three seconds, even the city seems to inhale. These intricate but seemingly irrelevant details are what make New York what it is: a city defined by constant motion, yet filled with hidden moments of stillness if you slow down enough to see them.

Millions of immigrants chasing new beginnings have arrived in New York, the City of Dreams—including my parents. Having known the hardship of finding employment in a foreign country, my parents believed the only way out of poverty was academic success. Throughout my childhood, they have always emphasized constant improvement, filling every spare moment with extracurriculars: online Chinese classes on Friday evenings, Art classes on Saturday mornings, Taekwondo twice a week, and monthly visits to the Buddhist temple. But in New York, there is always someone doing more: child prodigies who attend college at thirteen, teenage entrepreneurs who retired their parents at twenty, or distant cousins attending an Ivy League college. I could blame it on the city’s relentless momentum, the one that drives us towards these far-stretched standards, but I won’t. The same momentum has shaped who I am. I’ve learned to turn delayed train services into extra shut-eye time, to set time aside to video call my grandparents, and to forgive those who push past in crowded streets. Living in New York means learning to move fast enough to keep up, yet slow enough to notice what others miss. Sometimes I wondered why my parents chose New York instead of somewhere quieter—Florida, maybe, or California. Maybe it was because we had family here. Or maybe it was because New York mirrors the immigrant dream: fast, demanding, yet full of possibility. But somewhere between the rushing crowds and rare stillness, I’m learning that slowing down doesn’t mean I’m falling behind. It means I’m finally keeping pace with myself.
